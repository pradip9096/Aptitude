Quick Reference

\(n \div 0 = \text{Undefined}\)

\(0 \div n = 0\)

\(a \div 1 = a\)

‚ö° Division Exam Cheat Sheet

1. The Snapshot Definition

  • Concept: Splitting a total into equal groups.
  • The "Black Hole" Rule (Division by Zero):
    • \(n \div 0 = \text{Undefined}\) (Impossible).
    • \(0 \div n = 0\) (Always zero, provided \(n \ne 0\)).
  • The Sign Rule:
    • Same signs \((+) \div (+)\) or \((-) \div (-) = \textbf{Positive}\).
    • Different signs \((+) \div (-)\) or \((-) \div (+) = \textbf{Negative}\).

2. Speed-Math Shortcuts (Mental Math)

The "Double and Shift" (Divide by 5, 50, 500):

  • Technique: Dividing by 5 is hard; multiplying by 2 is easy. Double the number and move the decimal left one spot.
Example: \(240 \div 5 \rightarrow (240 \times 2) = 480 \rightarrow \mathbf{48}\).
Example: \(135 \div 5 \rightarrow 270 \rightarrow \mathbf{27.0}\).

The "Quadruple and Shift" (Divide by 25):

  • Technique: Multiply by 4 and move the decimal left two spots (divide by 100).
Example: \(300 \div 25 \rightarrow (300 \times 4) = 1200 \rightarrow \mathbf{12.00}\).

The "Zero Cancellation":

  • Technique: If both numbers end in zero, cross them out in pairs before dividing.
Example: \(4500 \div 90 \rightarrow 450 \div 9 = \mathbf{50}\).

The Factor Method:

  • Technique: If the divisor is a composite number (like 12), divide by its factors (3 and 4) step-by-step.
Example: \(156 \div 12 \rightarrow (156 \div 2) = 78 \rightarrow (78 \div 6) = \mathbf{13}\).

3. MCQ Elimination Tactics (Verification)

Strategy: Avoid Long Division unless absolutely necessary.

1. Inverse Multiplication (Back-Solving):

  • Instead of calculating \(1512 \div 42 = ?\), take the options (\(A: 30, B: 36, C: 40\)) and multiply them by 42.
  • Check: \(42 \times 30 \approx 1200\) (Too small). \(42 \times 40 \approx 1600\) (Too big). The answer must be 36.

2. Estimation (Compatible Numbers):

Round the numbers to the nearest easy multiples.

Example: \(312 \div 48\)
Estimate: \(300 \div 50 = \mathbf{6}\). Look for an option close to 6.

3. Last Digit Logic:

Multiply the last digit of the divisor by the last digit of the options. The result must match the last digit of the dividend.

Example: Dividend: \(\dots 4\), Divisor: \(\dots 8\).
Option A ends in 3: \(8 \times 3 = 24\) (Matches dividend ending in 4). Keep it.
Option B ends in 2: \(8 \times 2 = 16\) (Ends in 6). Eliminate.

4. Essential Formulas & Relationships

Concept Formula
The Master Division Formula \(\text{Dividend} = (\text{Divisor} \times \text{Quotient}) + \text{Remainder}\)
Use this to check answers or solve for missing variables.
Division of Fractions \(\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}\)
(Keep the first, Flip the second, Multiply).
Average \(\text{Sum of terms} \div \text{Number of terms}\)

5. Word Problem "Trigger Words"

If you see these phrases, you need to divide:

  • "Per" (e.g., miles per hour, cost per item).
  • "Shared equally among..."
  • "Split into..."
  • "Find the unit price..."
  • "Average of..."
  • "Ratio of A to B..."

6. The "Exam Trap" Alert ‚ö†Ô∏è

Trap 1: Rounding the Remainder context
  • Problem: "100 students need buses. Each bus holds 40. How many buses?"
  • Math: \(100 \div 40 = 2.5\).
  • Trap: Picking 2.5 or 2.
  • Fix: You cannot order half a bus. Round UP to 3.
Trap 2: Moving the decimal the wrong way (Divide vs Multiply)
  • Fix: Division makes numbers smaller (usually). If your answer got bigger when dividing by a whole number, you went the wrong way.
Trap 3: Dividing by Decimals (\(12 \div 0.3\))
  • Fix: Never divide by a decimal. Move the decimal point in both numbers until the divisor is a whole number (\(120 \div 3 = 40\)).
